Analytical Thinking & Formula 1

Formula 1 is not just motorsport — it is engineering, data science, strategy, and split-second decision-making compressed into 90 minutes. Following F1 has sharpened the way I think: I look for patterns, consider variables, and never accept the obvious answer as the only one.

Academic Impact: In networking labs, I approach problems the same way an F1 strategist approaches a race — systematically, with contingency plans. When a simulation fails, I analyse the data before changing anything. This approach has saved me hours of trial-and-error troubleshooting.

Chill Creativity & Home Cooking

Cooking is my go-to way to unwind. I'm not following a strict recipe — I just throw on some music, grab what's in the kitchen, and figure it out. Half the time it's for myself after a long day; other times I'm cooking for close friends who come through, and that's honestly when the best stuff happens. It's relaxed, personal, and just mine.

My Hero & Mentor

Lewis Hamilton

Racing Driver Seven-time F1 World Champion Icon

Background:
Sir Lewis Carl Davidson Hamilton (born 1985) is a British Formula 1 racing driver and the most decorated driver in the history of the sport. He grew up in Stevenage, England in a working-class family — his father Anthony worked three jobs to fund Lewis's karting career from age eight. Through sheer talent and relentless determination, Hamilton joined McLaren's F1 team at 22 and won his first World Championship in 2008, becoming the first Black driver to win the title.

He went on to claim six more championships with Mercedes (2014–2015, 2017–2018, 2019–2020), setting all-time records for race wins, pole positions, and podiums. Beyond racing, Hamilton is widely recognised for his outspoken advocacy on racial equality, sustainability, and inclusion — using his global platform to create real change both inside and outside of Formula 1.

"Still I Rise." – Lewis Hamilton (personal motto, inspired by Maya Angelou)

Why Hamilton Is My Hero:
What makes Hamilton extraordinary is where he started. He came from nothing — a working-class kid in a sport dominated by wealth and privilege — and didn't just compete, he rewrote the record books. He was told repeatedly that he didn't belong, and he answered every single time on the track.

As a Jamaican student pushing into a field where people like me aren't always expected to thrive, that story hits close to home. Hamilton showed me that the best response to doubt is results. He works harder than anyone, stays composed under pressure, and never lets his environment define his ceiling — all things I carry into my studies and my goals.

He also reminds me that success should stand for something. Hamilton uses his name to fight for equity and inclusion — proving that greatness and purpose can coexist. Whatever I build in cybersecurity, I want it to count for Jamaica and the people who come after me.

AI Businesses of 2025 – Report

After reviewing the blog on AI Businesses of 2025, my favourite idea from the list is AI-Powered Cybersecurity (Autonomous Threat Detection) — systems that use artificial intelligence to autonomously detect, analyse, and respond to cyber threats in real time, faster than any human security team could.

The Idea: AI-Powered Cybersecurity & Autonomous Threat Detection

How It Can Be Applied in My World

As a Computer Network & Security student, this idea lives at the intersection of everything I am studying. Traditional cybersecurity relies on human analysts reviewing alerts — but the volume of threats modern organisations face makes this impossible to manage manually. An AI system can monitor network traffic 24/7, identify anomalies in milliseconds, and automatically isolate infected systems before a breach spreads.

In a Jamaican context, government ministries and financial institutions are increasingly targeted by ransomware and phishing attacks, yet Jamaica has a critical shortage of trained cybersecurity professionals. AI-powered threat detection could provide enterprise-level security to organisations that cannot afford large security teams — a democratisation of digital defence.

Why It Would Be Useful

  1. Reduces response time from hours to milliseconds — containing breaches before they escalate.
  2. Operates continuously without fatigue, vacation days, or human error.
  3. Learns from each new threat, becoming more effective over time through machine learning.
  4. Allows smaller Caribbean organisations to access world-class security without full security teams.
  5. Frees human analysts to focus on high-level strategy rather than routine alert monitoring.

Ethical Issues & My Solutions

  • False Positives & Over-Blocking: AI systems can incorrectly flag legitimate activity, disrupting normal business operations. Solution: Implement a human-in-the-loop model where AI flags and isolates, but a human approves permanent blocks — balancing speed with accuracy.
  • Mass Surveillance Creep: Systems built to monitor networks can slide into monitoring employee behaviour and private communications. Solution: Strict legal frameworks defining what data the AI can access, with independent audits and transparent policies employees can read and challenge.
  • Algorithmic Bias: If trained on biased data, AI may disproportionately flag traffic from certain geographic regions. Solution: Mandate diverse training datasets and require third-party bias audits before deployment in public-sector systems.
  • Weaponisation: The same AI that defends can be trained to attack. Solution: International treaties governing offensive AI in cyberspace, paired with strong export controls on dual-use AI security tools.

Real-World Example

Darktrace is the world's leading AI cybersecurity company, using a self-learning AI modelled on the human immune system. It detects and responds autonomously to cyber threats across cloud, email, and network environments — all without predefined rules. Used by over 9,000 organisations globally, including hospitals, banks, and government agencies.
